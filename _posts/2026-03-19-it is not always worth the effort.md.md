**Shuwaki:**
What is your take on this — *"It is not always worth the effort to try to fix contempt with proving one's self"*

---

**Claude:**
This is a genuinely sharp observation, and I think it holds up well under scrutiny.

Contempt is fundamentally different from disagreement or even dislike. When someone holds you in contempt, they've already made a judgment about your *worth* — not just your actions or opinions. That's why proving yourself is such an exhausting and often futile response to it.

A few reasons why the statement rings true:

**The proving game is rigged.** When you attempt to prove yourself to someone who holds you in contempt, you're implicitly accepting their position as the judge. You've already conceded that their assessment of you *matters enough to contest*. This often reinforces the dynamic rather than resolving it — because now they see you as someone who needs their validation.

**Contempt is rarely about evidence.** Disagreements can be resolved with facts and demonstration. But contempt usually has emotional, social, or psychological roots — envy, tribalism, wounded pride, or prior narrative. New evidence rarely dismantles it because the contempt wasn't built on evidence in the first place.

**The cost is real.** The effort of constantly performing for a hostile audience takes a toll — on your energy, your self-perception, and sometimes your actual work. You end up optimizing for the wrong audience.

---

Where I'd add nuance though: *not always* is doing important work in that statement. There are cases where it *is* worth the effort:

- When the contemptuous person holds real power over outcomes you care about (a boss, a gatekeeper)
- When the contempt is based on a *specific, correctable misunderstanding* rather than a deep judgment
- When the relationship itself is worth preserving and the person is genuinely reachable

The real skill is discernment — knowing when you're dealing with correctable perception versus deep-seated contempt, and whether the person's opinion actually has leverage over anything that matters to you.

In most ordinary social situations though? The statement is right. Your energy is usually better spent among people who engage with you in good faith.

What's the context prompting this for you?

**Shuwaki:**
Some client asking a pharmacist to get an antimalarial drug, then calling their physician to find out whether the drug is good, yet asking the pharmacist for dosage. The pharmacist then quietly replied *"call your Dr."* — while discussing about the situation, somebody made that initial statement.

---

**Claude:**
Ah, that context makes the statement land even more precisely.

The pharmacist is dealing with a dynamic that many professionals face — a client who is simultaneously **seeking their expertise and undermining it**. The client calls the physician to verify what the pharmacist says, then turns around and asks the pharmacist for dosage. That's a contradictory posture — it signals *"I don't fully trust you"* while still expecting full service.

The quiet *"call your Dr."* is actually a composed and professional response. Here's why it reflects the original statement well:

**The pharmacist read the situation correctly.** When a client has already demonstrated they'll go over your head to verify your competence, explaining yourself or elaborating your dosage guidance doesn't resolve the distrust — it just gives them more material to double-check. Proving yourself to someone in that posture is largely performative labour.

**Withdrawing is not incompetence — it's boundary-setting.** By redirecting to the physician, the pharmacist isn't failing their duty. They're essentially saying: *you've already established that channel as your authority, so use it consistently.* It's clean and professional.

**The contempt here might be subtle but it's real.** It may not be malicious — it could come from health anxiety, lack of trust in pharmacists generally (which is unfortunately common, especially in contexts where pharmacy is undervalued as a profession), or just habit. But the effect is the same — the pharmacist's judgment is being treated as provisional until a "more qualified" person confirms it.

The irony of course is that **pharmacists are often the most immediately qualified person on drug interactions, dosing, and contraindications** — sometimes more so in practical terms than the prescribing physician. So the contempt carries an extra sting.

The original statement fits perfectly here — trying to prove oneself to that client in that moment would have been exhausting and probably pointless. The quiet redirect was the right call.
